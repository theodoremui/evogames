What is Game Theory?

Game theory is the study of mathematical models of strategic interactions among rational agents. It has applications in economics, political science, psychology, and biology. Game theory provides a framework for analyzing situations where multiple agents make decisions that affect each other's outcomes.

The key components of game theory include:

  • Players: The decision-makers in the game
  • Strategies: The possible actions available to players
  • Payoffs: The rewards or penalties received by players based on their actions
  • Information: What players know when making decisions

Prisoner's Dilemma

The Prisoner's Dilemma is one of the most famous scenarios in game theory. Two individuals are arrested for a crime. Each prisoner is offered the opportunity to betray the other by testifying that the other committed the crime.

The possible outcomes are:

  • If both prisoners betray each other (defect), both serve 2 years in prison
  • If one betrays (defects) and the other remains silent (cooperates), the betrayer goes free and the silent prisoner serves 3 years
  • If both remain silent (cooperate), both serve 1 year in prison

The dilemma arises because while mutual cooperation yields the best collective outcome, rational self-interest leads both players to defect.

Strategies

In repeated games like the Prisoner's Dilemma, players can adopt various strategies:

Cooperate on the first move, then copy your opponent's previous move. This strategy is forgiving but retaliatory.

Always cooperate regardless of what the opponent does. This strategy is vulnerable to exploitation.

Always defect regardless of what the opponent does. This strategy prevents exploitation but prevents mutual cooperation.

Only defects if the opponent defects twice in a row. More forgiving than Tit-for-Tat.

Defects twice if the opponent defects once. More retaliatory than Tit-for-Tat.

If you got a good outcome, repeat your move. If you got a bad outcome, change your move.

Cooperates until the opponent defects, then always defects thereafter. Unforgiving.

Randomly decides whether to cooperate or defect with equal probability.

Educational Use

This simulation platform can be used for various educational purposes:

  • Understanding the basic concepts of game theory
  • Exploring how different strategies perform in repeated games
  • Analyzing how the mix of strategies in a population affects outcomes
  • Investigating the evolution of cooperation
  • Demonstrating concepts like Nash equilibrium, Pareto optimality, and social dilemmas

Students can experiment with different configurations to answer questions like:

  • Which strategy performs best in a mixed population?
  • How does the number of rounds affect strategy performance?
  • What happens when cooperators face defectors?
  • How do different initial conditions affect the outcome?
